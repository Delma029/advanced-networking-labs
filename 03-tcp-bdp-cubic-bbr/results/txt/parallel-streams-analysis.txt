Parallel stream sweep, 30s each, same tuned link (buffers + tbf burst
128Kb from Phase 2):

Streams | Aggregate    | Retransmits
1       | 160 Mbit/s   | 68
2       | 536 Mbit/s   | 205
4       | 456 Mbit/s   | 324
8       | 163 Mbit/s   | 157
16      | 130 Mbit/s   | 150

NOT the classic "diminishing returns" curve where more streams keep
helping, just less each time. This peaks at 2 streams and then degrades
continuously through 16 -- negative returns past the first doubling.

Caveat: P1's 160 Mbit/s here is far below the 588 Mbit/s single-stream
result from the earlier 60s test. This 30s test window is shorter than
the ~8s ramp-up + steady-state duration CUBIC needed to reach its full
42MB window in the earlier test, so single-stream is disadvantaged here
by test duration, not by anything about the P1 condition itself. A fair
comparison would need equal duration across all stream counts, ideally
60s+ per run.

Why more streams hurts here: all streams compete for the same
tbf-shaped, single physical link with only 400ms of queue latency
budget. With many streams simultaneously growing their own Cwnd, they
collectively demand more than the queue can absorb -- retransmits climb
sharply (68 -> 205 -> 324 at 1/2/4 streams) even as per-stream and
aggregate throughput fall. This is small-scale congestion collapse:
loss-based backoff across many competing flows prevents any single flow
from building a meaningful window, unlike one flow that gets the whole
queue's growth budget to itself.

Real-world implication: parallel streams help when the constraint is
a SINGLE flow's own window-growth limitation on an otherwise-idle path
(e.g. bulk transfer tools using multiple TCP streams). They actively
hurt when the bottleneck is shared queue/shaper capacity being
contended by multiple flows -- exactly the case here. Which regime
applies depends entirely on where the actual bottleneck sits, not on a
universal "more streams = more throughput" rule.

CORRECTED 60-SECOND SWEEP (fair equal-duration comparison):

Streams | Aggregate    | Retransmits
1       | 522 Mbit/s   | 90
2       | 454 Mbit/s   | 54
4       | 561 Mbit/s   | 601
8       | 241 Mbit/s   | 125
16      | 136 Mbit/s   | 214

Revised conclusion: the 30-second sweep's "peaks at 2 streams" result
was an artifact of unfair test duration -- single-stream CUBIC hadn't
had time to reach its own 42MB steady-state window in only 30s. With
60s (enough time for single-stream to reach the 522 Mbit/s ceiling
already established in Phase 2), parallel streams provide NO real
benefit: 2 streams performs slightly worse than 1; 4 streams gains
only ~7% aggregate throughput at the cost of 6.7x more retransmissions
(601 vs 90); 8 and 16 streams collapse well below single-stream
performance.

This contradicts the "diminishing returns, more streams still help a
little" framing from the original project plan. The correct, more
precise conclusion: parallel streams help when a single flow cannot
grow its own window fast enough to use an otherwise-uncontended path
(the classic justification for tools like parallel-transfer utilities).
Here, single-stream CUBIC was ALREADY capable of reaching the link's
practical ceiling within the test window once given enough time --
so parallel streams add queue contention without solving any actual
problem, and pay for it in retransmissions.

Methodological note: the 30s vs 60s discrepancy is itself a finding --
test duration must be matched to the time a single flow needs to reach
steady state (established in Phase 2 as ~8s to break through, longer
to fully stabilize) before any stream-count comparison is fair. The
initial 30s sweep is kept in the record specifically because the
contrast with this corrected version demonstrates why.
