Parallel stream sweep, 30s each, same tuned link (buffers + tbf burst
128Kb from Phase 2):

Streams | Aggregate    | Retransmits
1       | 160 Mbit/s   | 68
2       | 536 Mbit/s   | 205
4       | 456 Mbit/s   | 324
8       | 163 Mbit/s   | 157
16      | 130 Mbit/s   | 150

NOT the classic "diminishing returns" curve where more streams keep
helping, just less each time. This peaks at 2 streams and then degrades
continuously through 16 -- negative returns past the first doubling.

Caveat: P1's 160 Mbit/s here is far below the 588 Mbit/s single-stream
result from the earlier 60s test. This 30s test window is shorter than
the ~8s ramp-up + steady-state duration CUBIC needed to reach its full
42MB window in the earlier test, so single-stream is disadvantaged here
by test duration, not by anything about the P1 condition itself. A fair
comparison would need equal duration across all stream counts, ideally
60s+ per run.

Why more streams hurts here: all streams compete for the same
tbf-shaped, single physical link with only 400ms of queue latency
budget. With many streams simultaneously growing their own Cwnd, they
collectively demand more than the queue can absorb -- retransmits climb
sharply (68 -> 205 -> 324 at 1/2/4 streams) even as per-stream and
aggregate throughput fall. This is small-scale congestion collapse:
loss-based backoff across many competing flows prevents any single flow
from building a meaningful window, unlike one flow that gets the whole
queue's growth budget to itself.

Real-world implication: parallel streams help when the constraint is
a SINGLE flow's own window-growth limitation on an otherwise-idle path
(e.g. bulk transfer tools using multiple TCP streams). They actively
hurt when the bottleneck is shared queue/shaper capacity being
contended by multiple flows -- exactly the case here. Which regime
applies depends entirely on where the actual bottleneck sits, not on a
universal "more streams = more throughput" rule.
