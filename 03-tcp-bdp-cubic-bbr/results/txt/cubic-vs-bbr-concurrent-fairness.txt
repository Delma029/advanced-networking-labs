Concurrent fairness test: CUBIC and BBR sharing the same bottleneck
link simultaneously, 60s, same tuned buffers, tbf shallow-queue link.

CUBIC (concurrent): 305 Mbit/s, 2 retransmits  -- ~87% of link
BBR   (concurrent): 43.4 Mbit/s, 0 retransmits -- ~13% of link

Comparison to solo runs (same link, same duration):
  CUBIC solo: 335 Mbit/s  -> concurrent: 305 Mbit/s (-9%, barely affected)
  BBR   solo: 305 Mbit/s  -> concurrent: 43.4 Mbit/s (-86%, nearly starved)

FINDING: CUBIC dominates BBR on this link, not the other way around --
the opposite of the commonly-cited "BBR starves CUBIC" result from
deep-buffer research contexts.

MECHANISM: this link's tbf queue is shallow (small burst/latency
limit). CUBIC is loss-based and keeps growing its window until a
packet actually drops, filling the shallow queue and holding it full.
BBR estimates available bandwidth from RTT; a queue kept full by CUBIC
inflates BBR's RTT samples, which BBR reads as "link saturated" and
throttles back -- even though the physical link has spare capacity,
it's just occupied by CUBIC's queue backlog. Zero retransmits on BBR's
side is consistent with this: BBR never even tries hard enough to
trigger loss, it backs off pre-emptively based on the (CUBIC-inflated)
delay signal alone.

IMPLICATION: fairness between loss-based and delay-based congestion
control is not a fixed property of the algorithms -- it depends
heavily on buffer depth at the bottleneck. Shallow buffers favor
CUBIC; deep buffers (per published BBR research) tend to favor BBR.
This lab's tbf configuration happens to sit on the shallow side.

--- Confirmation run (run 2) ---
CUBIC (concurrent): 375 Mbit/s, 17 retransmits -- ~81% of link
BBR   (concurrent): 90.6 Mbit/s, 0 retransmits -- ~19% of link

CONSISTENCY CHECK: pattern holds across both runs -- CUBIC took the
clear majority share in both (87% run 1, 81% run 2), and BBR recorded
zero retransmits in both runs, consistent with BBR backing off
pre-emptively on RTT/delay signal rather than ever pushing hard enough
to trigger a loss event. The exact split varied (87/13 vs 81/19),
which is expected run-to-run variance on this link -- already observed
earlier in solo CUBIC runs (335 vs 522-588 Mbit/s across different
sessions). The qualitative finding (CUBIC dominates, BBR retreats
substantially) is consistent; the precise ratio is not claimed to be a
fixed number, only "CUBIC took roughly 80-90% of the shared link
across two independent trials."
